Annual allowance for pension contributions
- Works for a given tax year (6th April to 5th April)
- Adjusted income = all forms of taxable income (not just salary) + any employee and employer pension contributions
- Threshold income = taxable income + employee pension contributions
    - If your threshold income is less than 200,000 your annual allowance will be 60,000
- You’ll have a reduced (‘tapered’) annual allowance in the current tax year if both:
    - your ‘threshold income’ is over £200,000
    - your ‘adjusted income’ is over £260,000
- Allowance is reduced by 1 pound for every 2 pounds of income above 260,000 until it reaches the minimum amount of 10,000
- You can carry over unused balances from the previous 3 tax years but only if you were registered in a pension scheme in those tax years
    - For me that means from 2025/2026 onwards

Example 
250,000 taxable income, 20,000 salary exchange pension contribution, 10,200 employer pension contribution, 20,000 savings and dividend income
- Threshold income = 250,000 + 20,000 + 20,000 = 290,000 > 200,000
- Adjusted income = 290,000 + 10,200 = 300,200
- Reduction in annual allowance = (300,200 - 260,000) / 2 = 20,100
- Tapered allowance =  60,000 - 20,100 = 39,900

Carry Forward Relief
- You can carry over allowance from the three previous tax years
- Only if you were a member of a UK-registered pension scheme during the relevant period
- For me that means starting in 2025/26
- To use the carry forward you must
    - Use all your annual allowance in the tax year in which you want to use the carry forward
    - You must use any unused annual allowance from the earliest tax year first
    - If you are subject to tapered annual allowance, you need to measure any unused annual allowance against your reduced allowance for each given tax year
    - To receive tax relief on your contributions, you must earn at least the amount you wish to contribute in the tax year you are making the contribution for (so if you want to contribute 100,000 in total you must earn at least 100,000 in that year). This does not apply if your employer is making the contribution on your behalf

Government gilts
The coupon on government gilts is counted as income
- So you will pay your marginal income tax rate on these
- But the capital gain made by selling the gilt at a higher price or holding it to maturity
- For that you would want to buy low-coupon gilts at a discount and hold them to maturity
- Be ware of clean vs dirty prices 
    - The 'clean' price of a bond (the one you'll see quoted on gilt price listings) is its price without accrued interest
    - This is because you may need to compensate the gilt's current holder for any coupon amount accrued – but not paid out to them – at the point you buy. 
    - The price you pay which incorporates this amount is known as the 'dirty' price.
    - If you buy a bond immediately after the latest coupon has been paid (usually this is every six months), the clean and dirty prices will be the same
    - There are calculators online to understand the implied annual yield
- This can probably amount to 3-4% a year of tax-free returns but with a lot of operational work to buy bonds every few months

Saving account, corporate bonds and foreign government bonds
- Treated like savings income
- If you exceeded your Personal Savings Allowance (which is 0 for additional rate payers), you pay your marginal income tax rate on the savings income
- So you would pay 45% tax on the income from foreign government bonds or interest from a savings account
